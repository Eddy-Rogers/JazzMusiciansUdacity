Influential Jazz Musicians of the 20th Century

Louis Armstrong(1901-1971)

Louis Armstrong was a famous jazz trumpeter and composer who changed American Jazz and American culture. He was well known for his compositions and his stage presence. However, what he is most well known for his involvement in desegregation. Armstrong was generally accepted into what was white culture in America in a time where America was very racially divided, which was almost unheard of for an African-American man. People often noted his cheerful and charismatic personality, which helped him develop friendships throughout his career, and made him a great entertainer. Well known pieces written by Armstrong include: "Swing That Music", "Gully Low Blues", and "Potato Head Blues"

Billie Holiday(1915-1959)

Billie Holiday was a well known Jazz singer and songwriter. She impacted the style of jazz singing in the 1930s-40s by implementing a style which was more similar to the instrumentation, using interesting phrsings and rythms. She was noted for playing with other important jazz figures such as Lester Young, Count Basie, and Artie Shaw. She was also a pioneer for womens rights and women in music. Although various women had begun to break the sex barriers in music, Holiday's position was particularly notable because she was not only a woman, but she was also African-American.

Thelonious Monk(1917-1982)

Thelonious Monk was a Jazz pianist and composer well known for his improvisation and composition of jazz standards. Monk wrote around 70 jazz pieces, a number second only to Duke Ellington, who wrote over 1000. Monk wrote standards such as "Blue Monk", "Straight, No Chaser", and "In Walked Bud". Monk was known not only as a band leader, but for his work with other leaders such as John Coltrane, and James Johnson. Monk was also known for his fashion in hats, suits, and sunglasses. An institute, The Thelonious Monk Institute of Jazz was created in his honor.

Charlie Parker(1920-1955)

Charlie Parker was an influential jazz saxophonist and composer accredited to the development and popularity of bebop, which is essentially a fast paced, melodically intricate sub-genre of jazz. Charlie Parker was considered a virtuoso, and blazed a trail for technique and skill on his instrument. In his early career, Parker practiced quite frequently, which allowed him to not only master his improvisational skills, but develop new techniques. Parker himself won a grammy in 1974 for "Best Performance by a Soloist", and multiple of his recordings earned grammies, including Billie's Bounce,Jazz at Massey Hall,Ornithology,and Charlie Parker with Strings.

Charles Mingus(1922-1979)

Charles Mingus was a jazz double bassist and composer known for his temper and long compositions. He was known for exhibiting displays of anger on stage, sometimes toward his band members, and sometimes toward the audience. because of this, he became known as "The angry man of jazz". Mingus was also know for his compositions which were primarily geared toward big band. His longest composition, entitled "epitaph" takes over 2 hours to perform.

John Coltrane(1926-1967)

John Coltrane was a Jazz saxophonist and composer who played during the bebop and hard bop era of jazz music. Although he was the band leader for over 50 recording sessions, he was well known for being a side musician for famous band leaders such as Miles Davis and Thelonious Monk. With Davis, he worked on albums Kind Of Blue and Milestones. John Coltrane is most famous as a leader for his album Blue Train.

Miles Davis(1926-1991)

Miles Davis was one of the most well known jazz musicians of all time. He was a composer and trumpet player, but he was most well known as a band leader. His 2 most popular albums were Kind of Blue and Milestones. Kind of Blue is regarded as Davis' masterpiece, featuring 5-star reviews on Allmusic and Entertainment Weekly, among other 5 star reviews. In 2003, Rolling Stones magazine ranked it number 12 in the list of 500 greatest albums of all time.
